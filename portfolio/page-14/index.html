Stitchworms

Stitchworms began as a class project investigating ritual—specifically, I wanted to learn how superstition functions as a behavioral tool—and whether a new, intentionally designed superstition could be created to guide care and attention.

At the time, while serving as the sewing machine technician in the Industrial Design department, I saw firsthand that most “broken” machines weren’t malfunctioning at all, but victims of rushed threading, inattention, and stressed students pushing too hard.

This revealed a core problem: students didn’t just need better instructions, they needed a way to slow down, reset, and treat the machines with care rather than urgency.

A classmate introduced the Taiwanese superstition of Kuai Kuai chips—snacks left beside machines to ensure good luck—which reframed my thinking around how playful rituals could influence technical systems.

I began prototyping with a squishy, low-stakes stuffed animal to test whether tactility and humor could anchor a ritual students would actually engage with.

Placing the prototype in graduate studios with a simple sign—“Squish for good luck”—I observed that students not only interacted with it, but began asking how the ritual could be expanded into something with story and meaning.

This feedback evolved into the Stitchworms: soft creatures paired with sewing machines that require care and attention, redirecting focus away from frustration and back toward calm, deliberate making.

To this day, the Stitchworms function as both superstition and system—encouraging slower sewing, better machine care, and providing embedded troubleshooting support for students working late into the night.
